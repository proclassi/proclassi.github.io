1. achievements of peter drucker​


2014 Lifetime Achievement Award Winner

Peter Drucker's (1909 - 2005) passion for good management practices came naturally. Growing up in a suburb of Vienna, Austria, he was exposed to leading intellectuals who frequented weekly soirées at the Drucker family home, and he learned about the dark side of leadership when two of his early essays were banned by the Nazi's – prompting his relocation to the UK and then to the U.S. He understood deeply that "management affects people and their lives" and dedicated himself to writing, teaching, and influencing leaders for the good of organizations and society.

As Rosabeth Moss Kanter pointed out in 2009 in the Harvard Business Review, three recurrent themes run through Drucker's 39 books and numerous articles: Management is a profession whose professionals' primary purpose should be the long-term health of organizations, which includes the consideration of social well-being, not just profit; employees in an information society require a purpose – other than earning money – for their work; and nonprofits are critical in creating a healthy society in which business can thrive. Known or his uncanny ability to predict significant societal shifts, a vast network of people continues to apply his principles to organizations today.

4. According to Peter Drucker," The entrepreneur always searches for changes, responds to it, and exploits it as an oppoortunity". As a future entrepreneur, cite specific business opportunity that you want to engage with and explain further to apply the concept of Drucker. ()


In its most basic form, a business opportunity is a packaged business investment that enables the buyer to launch a business. Technically speaking, all franchises are business opportunities, but not all franchises are business opportunities. In contrast to a franchise, the seller of a business opportunity normally has no influence over the buyer's activities. In fact, after a sale is made, there is typically no ongoing interaction between the seller and the buyer in business opportunity programs.

Steps for Generating New Business Opportunities from the Entrepreneurs:

1. Concentrate on your main product.

In small service businesses, it's typical for the owner to believe that in order to compete, they must do what the "big boys" do. The fact is that small business owners can never truly compete on an equal footing. Therefore, it's crucial for small firms to stand out from the competition by emphasizing the distinctive qualities and key items that they offer customers. The key to success for an entrepreneur is specialization.

2. Don't complicate your pitch.

Every business should have a "elevator pitch," which is a brief message that can be delivered to a prospect in no more than 30 seconds. It describes the benefits your product or service offers so the prospect may see how it applies to them.

3. Hold fast to your identity.

You will achieve your goals if you are aware of who you are and what excites (and bores) you. Procrastination and discomfort are the two things that can sabotage growth plans the most; it's in our tendency to put off dealing with things that make us uncomfortable. And every business owner hates the multitude of everyday obligations they must meet. It's time to start delegating if you find yourself procrastinating.

4. Map it.

Your service plan can be determined by matching your capabilities to the needs of your target clients. Even though you may be quite knowledgeable in many different areas, you'll discover that you are pursuing clients that don't require your specific field of knowledge.

5. Use marketing tools that are most effective for you.

Choose a marketing plan that complements both your personality and the target audience you are trying to reach. For instance, don't book yourself to participate on a panel in the hopes of generating revenue if you're frightened of speaking in front of a public. If you don't perform well, your efforts will be unsuccessful.

6. Put a plan of action into action.

We have been in the planning phase up to this point; it is now time to get down to business. You can utilize the road map provided by your action plan to track your progress. Identify objectives that can be checked off at three and six months. Check your strategy periodically over each three-month period to evaluate if you're accomplishing your objectives.

7. Implement the plan.

The final step is very simple: Just do it. After completing the daily tasks, add something additional to your success plan. In order to keep on track, if approaching your plan gives you the willies, either get over it or replace it with a comfortable action. Don't let unforeseen duties consume time that could be spent working toward your goal. Most importantly, enjoy the process.
