3. Activity 2: Nothing but Propagandas! Directions: To check the depth of your understanding, you will be answering the activities provided. In your own words, describe how the following propaganda techniques are used by advertisements and personalities you know. Give example to explain your answer. Use the space provided for you. Note, that you have to be careful not to use false accusation. Propaganda Technique 1 Name calling 2 Card stacking 3 Soft soap 4 Plain folks 5 Glittering generalities Explanation/Example


Answer:

Name calling is a propaganda technique where a person or a product is given a negative label in order to make it less attractive to the audience. This is often used in politics where candidates will call their opponents with negative names to discredit them. For example, in a political campaign, a candidate might refer to their opponent as a "liar" or a "cheater" to create a negative perception in the minds of the voters.

Card stacking is a propaganda technique where only positive information is presented while negative information is suppressed. Advertisements often use this technique to highlight only the positive features of their product while ignoring any negative aspects. For example, an advertisement for a car might highlight its fuel efficiency and performance but not mention its high price.

Soft soap is a propaganda technique where the audience is presented with information that is overly positive or flattering. Advertisements often use this technique to create a positive image of their product in the minds of consumers. For example, an advertisement for a beauty product might claim that it will make you look 10 years younger and give you flawless skin, even if the product has no scientific backing to support these claims.

Plain folks is a propaganda technique where the speaker presents themselves as a regular, down-to-earth person, just like the audience they are trying to appeal to. This technique is often used in political campaigns where candidates try to connect with voters by presenting themselves as an "ordinary person." For example, a politician might wear jeans and a t-shirt while giving a speech to appear relatable to their audience.

Glittering generalities is a propaganda technique where vague, positive-sounding statements are used to appeal to the emotions of the audience without providing any concrete information. Advertisements often use this technique to make their product sound appealing without actually providing any real benefits. For example, an advertisement for a soft drink might claim that it is "refreshing" and "energizing" without providing any actual information about what is in the drink.
