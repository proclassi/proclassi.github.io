10. women oppression in the movie enola Holmes ​


Answer:

Explanation:

The movie "Enola Holmes" explores the theme of women's oppression through the character of Enola Holmes, the younger sister of Sherlock Holmes. Set in Victorian England, the film portrays the societal norms of the time that limited the opportunities available to women and treated them as inferior to men.

Enola's mother, who raised her in isolation and taught her unconventional skills such as self-defense and deduction, disappears suddenly, prompting Enola to embark on a journey to find her. Throughout the movie, Enola faces various forms of oppression, including:

Limited education opportunities: Enola's brothers, Sherlock and Mycroft, believe that women's education should be limited to sewing and etiquette, rather than academic subjects like science and math.

Forced marriage: Enola's mother had arranged a marriage for her, which she was expected to comply with. However, Enola resists this idea and chooses her own path.

Invisibility and dismissal: Enola is often dismissed and overlooked by male characters in the movie, who underestimate her intelligence and capability due to her gender.

Physical violence: Enola experiences physical violence and assault when she is attacked by a man who is threatened by her intelligence and resourcefulness.

Through Enola's journey, the movie challenges the patriarchal norms of Victorian England and highlights the importance of women's empowerment and the fight against gender-based oppression.

17. Women talk two muhc by janet holmes


Janet Holmes discusses how many people automatically believe that women speak too much and more than men do in "Women Talk Too Much." Through the use of studies, Janet Holmes aims to show that this claim is inaccurate. She cites a study by Deborah James and Janice Drakich, two Canadian researchers, as an illustration. According to Holmes, the answer to the question "do women talk more than males" is neither yes nor no; rather, it depends on a variety of circumstances. such as social variables and the subjects they like to discuss more.

In situations when they feel comfortable and are attempting to forge personal connections, Holmes finds that women talk more than men.

When supporting and facilitating a topic, women often speak. However, in places like business meetings and classrooms, where men predominate the discourse, women tend to speak less. When attempting to be instructive and referential, men tend to talk more. Holmes discusses the various factors in tasks, such as social confidence. Women avoid speaking in the classroom since it is perceived as a "show-off" act and attracts criticism.

Inside the sanctum of 221B Baker Street, Holmes and Watson existed in a fantasy of late Victorian homosociality, a cross between Oxbridge undergraduate life and feckless bohemia. Their world was incomplete, yet perfect, like all fantasies, as bewitching for what it excluded—regular hours, real jobs, sexuality and its discontents—as for the pipe smoke, disguises, and idle gunplay that it contained. Arthur Conan Doyle got tired of it all long, long before his fans did, and married Watson off to a woman who mattered so little to her creator that he could not remember if she were alive or dead, and he killed off Holmes himself in 1893. Ten years later, popular demand obliged Conan Doyle to resurrect the great detective, whereupon Watson moved right back into the Baker Street flat.
